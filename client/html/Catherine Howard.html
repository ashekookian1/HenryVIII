Catherine Howard: 1525 - 1542





Childhood and Early Life: 1525 - 1540

Birth: 1525 (exact date unknown) in Lambeth, a district in London.

Parents: Lord Edmund Howard (brother to Elizabeth Howard - Anne Boelyn's mother - thus making her a first cousin of Anne) and Joyce Culpeper.

Childhood: She was one of six children. Her mother died in 1528. Her father was borne to financial problems so her education was not as expansive as Anne Boleyn's and Katherine of Aragon's, but she was taught to read and write, though she was barely literate.

1531: Because of her father's financial problems, and her mother's death, her father was not able to care for his family, so he had Catherine and her brother Henry were sent to stay in the household of Agnes Howard, the Dowager Duchess of Norfolk and their step-grandmother. The duchess ran a household that consisted of many wards in need of care.



1536 - 1538: Catherine at 11, still living in the Duchess's household, is given music lessons by Henry Manox, who is probably in his early to mid-twenties. They have an affair, though when later questioned by Archbishop Cranmer, she would say it wasn't consummated.

1538 - 1539: Still at the Duchess's household, Catherine now 13, has an affair with the Duchess's secretary, Francis Dereham, a man in his early-thirties. They address each other as "husband" and "wife" and possibly agreed to get married in the future. As the affair was consummated, if they had exchanged vows before this, then they would have been legally married in the eyes of the English Church. Dereham leaves to make his fortune in Ireland in 1539.

Spring 1540: Her uncle, the Duke of Norfolk, arranges for her to come to court.



Marriage to Henry VIII: July 1540 - February 1542

April 1540: Henry starts an affair with Catherine.



July 9th, 1540: As Henry is now divorced from Anne of Cleeves, he intends to marry Catherine.

July 28th 1540: Henry marries Catherine at Oatlands Palace. They spend 10 days alone in the palace, to enjoy some time together, and also before he has to introduce her to court and all the duties she will have to deal with as Queen.

August 8th, 1540: Catherine is declared Queen of England.



Life as Queen of England: Henry at 49, is besotted by the 15 year-old Catherine and showers her with costly gifts, including furs, land, castles, manors and jewels. She is not very interested in him due to the age difference, but enjoys the gifts nonetheless. He calls her his "rose without a thorn" as he thinks he has finally found the perfect wife. However, she did show some kindness to his subjects, and persuaded him to release two prisoners held in the Tower of London on minor charges. She also sent another prisoner held there, an elderly lady, some warm clothes. For these actions, she gained approval at court.

1541: Most likely due to the age difference between her and the king, and her lack of attraction to him, Catherine starts an affair with a servant of Henry's, and a distant cousin from her mother's side, that is closer to her age: Thomas Culpeper. Her lady in waiting Jane Boleyn (wife of the executed George Boleyn) helps ward off the king by acting as a lookout in front of the door when Catherine and Thomas are together.

August 27th 1541: Catherine's former lover Francis Derham blackmails her into getting employment in her court as her private secretary and usher of her chamber. (Henry thinks that she was chaste before she met him, and she does not want to dispel this illusion and a possible divorce.)

Summer 1541: Mary Lascelles, a girl who had lived with Catherine when she was younger in the house of her step-grandmother, and knew of her past liasons with Henry Manox and Francis Derham, is angry at not getting a post in Catherine's household. She tells all of this to her brother John when he, knowing that she knew Catherine, asks why she doesn't want to ask her for a position in her household. John, a Protestant, now sees an opportunity to take down the Catholic Queen Catherine. John and Mary go to Archbishop Cranmer about their allegations. Cranmer writes everything down and gives a letter to Henry of his findings.

November 1st, 1541: Henry gets the letter about Cranmer's findings and orders him to investigate Catherine's conduct further. Henry has Catherine confined to house arrest, dissmissing all her ladies and leaving only Jane Boleyn with her.

November 22nd, 1541: Based on all the evidence that Cranmner has found after questioning her servants, including now finding out about her affair with Culpeper and Jane Boleyn's role in it, Henry strips Catherine of her title of Queen.

December 1st, 1541: Derham and Culpepper were found guilty of treason and scheduled to be executed.

December 10th, 1541: To make a point about about committing adultery with the Queen of England, Derham and Culpepper were hanged, cut down alive, then drawn and quartered and finally, beheaded. Their heads were displays on spikes on London Bridge until at least 1546.

February 7th, 1542: Henry gets Parliament to pass a bill of attainder against Catherine and Jane Boleyn, charging them for treason in the roles they played in Catherine's affair. The penalty for this is death.

February 13th, 1542: Catherine and Jane are beheaded for treason at the Tower of London. Catherine is buried next to her cousin Anne Boleyn in the chapel of St. Peter Ad Vicula in the Tower.
