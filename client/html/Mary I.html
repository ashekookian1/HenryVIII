Mary I: 1516 - 1558





Childhood and Early Life: 1516 - 1528

Birth: February 18th, 1516 at the Palace of Greenwich, London.

Mother: Katherine of Aragon - Henry's first wife.

Childhood: As was the custom at the time, Mary wasn't raised in the same palace that her father was living in. Instead, she was given her own household on one of her father's properties. This was done not only so Henry could focus on his royal duties, but also because of the high risk of disease and infant death at the time. Therefore, she would be kept out of court, and lessen her chances of catching disease and dying prematurely. Her household consisted of a wet nurse, (until her services were no longer needed a year into Mary's life) cleaning staff and cooks among others.

Education: She was tutored in French, Latin, Spanish, music, dance and possibly Greek.



1525 - 1528: As Mary was an only child and had no surviving brothers who would have become Prince of Wales, in 1525, Henry sent Mary to the border of Wales to preside, presumably in name only, over the Council of Wales and the Marches. She was given her own court based at Ludlow Castle in Wales and many of the royal prerogatives normally reserved for a Prince of Wales. In the three years spent in the Welsh Marches, she would make regular visits home to her father's court, before returning permanently to the home counties around London in mid-1528.





Her Mother's Divorce and Death: 1526 - 1536

Spring 1526: Henry starts an affair with Lady Anne Boleyn. She is the younger sister of his former mistress Mary Boleyn, and also a lady in waiting to Katherine of Aragon. Henry was attracted to her, due to her intelligence, wit, love of art and music, and fashion sense. He asked her to be his only mistress and she refused, asking to be his wife instead.



1527-8: As Henry's desire to marry Anne and their affair is made public, the court starts paying tribute to her, and Katherine is left out. Henry uses the excuse that the Pope had no legal basis to give him a dispensation to allow him to marry Katherine, as she was his brother's widow. Henry says that as there is a biblical passage against that, the pope had no authority to contravene it.

May 31st - July 23rd 1529: Henry is able to get the Pope to send a papal legate to London to try his divorce case based on his claim that the Pope wasn't allowed to issue the dispensation. The legate: Cardinal Campeggio sets up a tribunal and listens to both sides of the argument. However, he is instructed by the Pope to recall the trial back to Rome, as Campeggio says that it can only be heard by the Pope. The reason for this is the Pope was under pressure from Holy Roman Emperor and King of Spain Charles V, also Katherine's nephew, not to allow the divorce to proceed.



February 7th, 1531: Henry, tired of waiting for the Pope's decision, and fearing that it will be against him, decides to take matters into his own hands. He calls the British clergy into the halls of Parliament and asks them to acknowledge him as the supreme head of the Church of England, refusing the Pope any authority over the English church. They grant him this on 11th.

July 14th, 1531: Henry banishes Katherine from court and sends her to live in his lesser castles. He is now seen at all court/social events with Anne Boleyn and not Katherine. Mary is no longer permitted to see her mother, as Henry is scared that they will conspire together to overthrow him due to their anger at the divorce. Mary will never again see Katherine in her lifetime.



August 22, 1532: Archishop Warham of Canterbury, the head of the English church and a religious conservative, dies, as is replaced by Henry with Thomas Cramner, a liberal cleric who supports and can grant the King's divorce.



September 1st, 1532: Henry now makes Anne Marquess of Pembroke, giving her a higher noble rank, so when he marries her, it would be considered a marriage of royalty and someone of an acceptable (high) noble rank.

January 25, 1533: Anne realizes that she is pregnant, and that is used as a reason to conduct a secret wedding between her and Henry. Accoring to the law about royal succession (it still applies to the royal family today), all royal children must be conceived in wedlock to be able to succeed to the throne; that's why the marriage was kept a secret.

April 9th, 1533: Katherine is informed by Henry's messengers that she is no longer Queen, and can only call herself Dowager Princess of Wales (as she was Arthur's widow).

April 12th, 1533: Anne now appears in court as Queen and on the next day, Easter, she is publicly declared Queen in all the churches in England.



May 23rd, 1533: Cranmer after convening an ecclesistical court, declares Henry's marriage to Katherine "null and void", due to Henry's original argument that the Pope didn't have the authority to grant the dispensation allowing Henry to marry Katherine after she had previously married his brother.

May 28th, 1533: Cranmer now pronounces Henry's marriage to Anne as "good and valid" legitimizing her position as Queen, and her soon to be child with Henry would now be considered legitimate and as a heir to the throne.

June 1st, 1533: Anne is crowned Queen of England.

September 7th, 1533: Birth of Henry and Anne's daughter: Elizabeth - the future Elizabeth I.



October 1st, 1533: Mary is informed that she is no longer a princess, and will now be called only Lady Mary, instead of princess, and in December, to emphasize this, is sent to serve as a lady in waiting to her half sister Elizabeth.

March 23rd, 1534: Parliament passes the Act of Succession, saying that only children born to Henry and Anne would be eligible to be heirs to the throne. It also says that anyone denying that Anne and Henry were legally married, or that she was legally Queen of England, or that Henry was now the head of the Church of England, would be committing treason and subject to execution. This results in quite a few people refusing to take the oath, and being subsequently beheaded. On the same day, the Pope declares Henry's marriage to Katherine and their children legitimate and orders him to banish Anne from court and reinstate Katherine in her place under pain of Henry's excommunication. Henry refuses, thus reinforcing the permanent break of the English Church from Rome (as is still the case today).

Treatment by Henry from 1534 - 6: Katherine and Mary, despite pressure over numerous occasions by Henry, refuse to take the oath for obvious reasons. Unlike others, Henry is scared to have them beheaded for this, as there is always the fear that if he does, the King of Spain and Holy Roman Emperor, Charles V, will invade England in order to defend the honor of his aunt.

December 1535: Katherine is bedridden with what would posthumously be found to be heart cancer.

January 7th, 1536: Katherine dies after dictating a farewell letter to Henry and signing it defiantly as Katherine the Queen, and not as Pricess Dowager of Wales as Henry wishes her to be now known as.

January 29th, 1536: Katherine was buried in Peterborough Cathedral as a Dowager Princess of Wales, and not a queen. Henry did not attend the funeral and forbade Mary to attend.

May 19th, 1536: Anne Boleyn is beheaded at Tower Green on false charges of committing adultery with five men, including her own brother. The charges were made up as Henry had gotten tired of Anne, wasn't happy that she hadn't given him a son, and was looking for an excuse to be rid of her and marry Jane Seymour - Anne's lady in waiting whom he was currently having an affair with.

May 30th, 1536: Jane Seymour and Henry are married and she is proclaimed Queen on June 4th.



June 8th, 1536: Second Act of Succession - saying that Henry's children with Anne Boleyn and Katherine of Aragon were illegitimate, and any heirs to the throne would come from children he had with Jane Seymour.

June 13th, 1536: Mary, realizing that now that her mother is gone, and Charles V is not interested in invading England to defend her own right to the throne, finally signs her agreement to the Second Act of Succession, agreeing that her parents marriage was invalid, and she is therefore illegitimate.

July 6th, 1536: Now that Mary had signed the Act, Henry decided to go and see her with Jane. Jane, a supporter of the Catholic cause, and wanting to see Mary reinstated as heir to the throne, had been asking Henry to reconcile with Mary. Henry refused to see her until Mary signed the Act. Once this was done, Jane urged Henry to publicly reconcile with Mary.

December 17th, 1536: Mary is received back at court by Jane and Henry, where he publicly reconciles with her, thanks to Jane's influence. After this, Mary is often at court and becomes close again with Jane, and is accorded precedence as the most important lady in court after her. Jane also tries to get Henry to reinstate Mary to the line of succession, but Henry refuses. This would not happen until his sixth wife, Katherine Parr, would take up the cause in the 1540s. Now that she is reconciled with her father, he gives her a few countryside houses, and also a household with staff should she want to live either at her outside houses, or at court.



Intervening Years: 1536 - 1553

1536 - 1543: Henry VIII marries another three times, and Mary is sometimes back at court to visit her stepmothers.

February 7th, 1544: As a thank you to Katherine Parr (his sixth wife) for bringing him closer to his children, Henry has Parliament pass the Third and final Act of Succession of his reign. It reinstated Mary and then Elizabeth (though not their former rank as princesses) and any children they might have to the line of succession after Edward and any children he might have.



January 28th, 1547: Henry VIII dies at age 55. Edward, Mary's half brother, at age 9, is now proclaimed King Edward VI. Henry is buried on February 16th at Windsor Castle next to his former wife Queen Jane (Edward'd mother) at his request.



1547 - 1553: For most of Edward's reign, Mary remained on her own estates and rarely attended court as she knew that she disagreed with him on religious matters. Mary was a Catholic, and Edward had been raised Protestant. Not only that, but with the help of his regency council and Archbishop Cranmer, he was slowing passing laws to make the official religious doctrine of the Church of England Protestant.



Edward VI's Death and Succession Crisis: 1553

June 1553: Knowing that he was terminally ill (probably of TB) the regency council persuaded Edward to make his will. The will, knowing that his successor would be his half sister Mary, a Catholic, sought to exclude her from the line of succession. It said that the throne would go to Lady Jane Grey , a Protesant, thus keeping England Protestant, and her male children. Jane Grey was the oldest grandchild of Mary Tudor, younger sister of Henry VIII. Jane was also conveniently married to Lord Guilford Dudley, a son of John Dudley. This way, John thought that he could keep control over the throne.





Explanation of Will: Henry's will had stated that if his children didn't have heirs, he wanted their successors to come from the line of his younger sister Mary, discounting the line of his older sister Margaret. (Tradition of male preference primogeniture (how the succession was determined at the time) dictated that it was all male children and their descendants in birth order, followed by all female children and their descendants, again by birth order.) This was because Margaret had married King James IV of Scotland, and therefore, her descendants would inherit the united crowns of Scotland and England. Henry didn't want this, because he had tried to conquer Scotland by force and failed. So if Margaret's children inherited the throne, he saw this as Scotland's revenge, and didn't want it. So in this way, Henry VIII's will was followed, but it skipped over the claims of Edward's sisters Mary and Elizabeth first, before moving on to his aunt's descendants.

July 6th, 1553: Edward dies at 15. He is buried on August 8th at Westminster Abbey.

July 10th, 1553: John Dudley proclaims Jane Grey Queen of England, after taking a few days to gather up his forces. Mary, Edward's sister, hearing that something like this was about to happen, goes to her estates in Norfolk, where she can count on the support of her tenants. She also proclaims herself Queen.

July 14th, 1553: John Dudley leaves London and takes his army with him out to Framlingham Castle in Suffolk where Mary and her loyal forces are. They number 20,000 vs. John's 3,000, as his declaring of Jane Queen proved very unpopular with the English people. Though some didn't like Mary based on her Catholicism, most supported her claim to the throne based on the English laws of succession regardless of her religion.

July 19th, 1553: The ruling Privy Council in London, realizing what was going on, and realizing they were headed for defeat, proclaimed Mary as queen, and told Dudley's army, still not yet arrived to meet Mary's to stop, and for Dudley to proclaim Mary as Queen. He did so.



July 24th, 1553: Dudley is arrested for his part in the revolt. He is imprisoned in the Tower of London along with Jane.



Regin as Queen Mary I: 1553 - 1558

August 3rd 1553: To celebrate her victory: Mary rode triumphantly into London on a wave of popular support, accompanied by her half-sister Elizabeth and a procession of over 800 nobles and gentlemen.

August 22nd 1553: Dudley is beheaded for treason for his part in supporting Jane Grey. Jane Grey and her husband, Guilford Dudley are found guilty, but left as prisoners in the Tower of London, as Mary realizes that they were John Dudley's pawns in the scheme to get Jane Grey to be queen, and didn't orchestrate anything themselves.

September 1553: Mary imprisons prominent Protestant clergy members including Archbishop Cranmer.

October 1st, 1553: Bishop Gardiner (she had newly released him from prison under Edward VI due to his Catholic beliefs - she also did this to Catholic politcians and clergy at the start of her reign) crowned Mary at Westminster Abbey.

Early October 1553: Mary declares her parents' marriage to be valid and restores the English church to Catholicism.

December 7th 1553: Mary wanting to marry a Catholic prince, so she could have children with him and keep the line of succession Catholic and away from Elizabeth, a Protestant, presented the Privy Council a marriage treaty saying that she wanted to marry Prince Philip of Spain. He was the heir to the throne of Spain and the Holy Roman Empire, and Mary's first cousin once removed.



January - February 1554: Wyatt's Rebellion Thomas Wyatt, a poet and popular courtier decides to rise up against Mary's proposed marriage. Most of the English people are behind him in thought, as they don't like Mary marrying a foreign prince, they prefer her to marry someone English. They fear that Philip would not only keep England Catholic again, but bring over the Spanish Inquisition to torture English Protestants. They do not like that Mary has instuted pro-Catholic reforms and imprisoned prominent Protestant clergy. Finally, they thought that Philip would take over England when he became King of Spain, and make it a satellite state of Spain.



Actual Rebellion: Wyatt gathered up some supporters, about 2,500, and planned to attack London. However, they were outnumbered by Mary's much larger forces and captured and executed for treason. Among the people who joined Wyatt and were execured were Henry Grey, Duke of Suffolk, and Jane Grey's father. Elizabeth, though protesting her innocence in the Wyatt affair, was questioned and imprisoned in the Tower of London for two months, then put under house arrest at Woodstock Palace.

February 12th, 1554: Despite not taking part in the rebellion, Jane Grey and Guilford Dudley were executed for treason, as if they were to live, they would be seen as rallying personages for the Protestant cause, and thus jepordize Mary's safety.

July 25th, 1554: Mary and Philip are married at Winchester Cathedral. Mary is 38 and Philip is 27. Age difference marriages were common at the time, as marriage was considered a move to advance your political and social standing, and not for romantic reasons. Philip lives with her at the English court at this time, and is declared King consort of England. (The only one ever in British history, the other husbands of queens regent were made princes only, not kings.)



September 1554 - July 1555: Mary exhibited signs of what she thought was pregnancy, (Elizabeth was called out of house arrest in April 1555 to witness the birth of a child that would stop her from becoming queen and turning England Protestant again) but was later found to be a false alarm. Most likely early symptoms of the stomach cancer that would later kill her.

January 16th, 1555: After reaching out to the Pope and informing him that she was reuniting the English church with him, Mary had Parliament pass the Heresy Acts, which said that Protesants could be burned at the stake for their beliefs. The first burnings started in February. Mary burned 280 people at the stake over the course of her reign, (including Thomas Cranmer in 1556 - who recanted his Protestantism, so as to free himself from imprisonment, but Mary didn't believe him and burned him anyway - it turned out she was right, as he changed his mind about the recanting right before he was burned at the stake) and therefore earned the nickname: Bloody Mary.

August 1555: Philip leaves England to command his armies against France in Flanders.

January 1556: Philip becomes King of Spain and the Holy Roman Empire after his father abdicates, and decides to go there to take up his throne.

March - July 1557: Philip returned to England to persuade Mary to support Spain in a war against France. Mary sent troops to help Spain in June. After this, Philip doesn't return to England in Mary's lifetime.

January 1558: In revenge for Mary sending help to Spain, the French take back Calais. The only English occupied city in France after the end of the hundred years war.

November 17th, 1558: Mary dies, age 42, probably of stomach cancer. Elizabeth, her half sister, is now Elizabeth I, Queen of England.

December 14th, 1558: Mary is buried in Westminster Abbey. James I (Elizabeth's successor) would later move her tomb to share it with Elizabeth's (also in the abbey) after her death as he placed his mother Mary's tomb in a prominent position in the abbey, and needed to rearrange things to make space.
