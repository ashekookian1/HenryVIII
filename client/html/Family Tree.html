Family Tree





Notes on the Family Tree:
1. Names in bold, for example: Henry VIII, Mary I, etc. imply Kings and Queens of England.
2. Spouses of people in the tree are not included due to space constraints.
3. James VI and James I is the same person. He was first King James VI of Scotland, and when Queen Elizabeth I of England passed away without children, he also became King James I of England; hence why only the name James I is bolded, and not James VI.





Explanation of the Normal English Line of Succession to the Throne in the 1500s: Henry VIII was the surviving son of Henry VII. Though he did have an older sister: Margaret, the law of succession at the time was for male preference primogeniture (only changed to absolute primogeniture in 2015 – meaning birth order only, regardless of gender), this meant that all sons in birth order, had preference over daughters. After that, it was all daughters in birth order.

So that is why, despite Edward VI being the youngest of Henry VIII’s children, he reigned first, and his older sisters reigned after him. So, in terms of the laws of succession at the time, if Henry’s children died childless (which did end up happening) then the next person in the line of succession would come from the line of his older sister Margaret. So ultimately the rules were followed, and after Elizabeth I died childless, she was succeeded by James VI of Scotland, who also became James I of England, thus forever reuniting the crowns of Scotland and England (still the case today).



How Henry Wanted to Alter the Succession: However, Henry wanted to alter his will so that after Elizabeth I’s death, the throne would bypass Margaret and go to his younger sister Mary and her descendants. He did this because he didn’t want the thrones of England and Scotland to be united. This was because Margaret had married King James IV of Scotland, but her son, James V, (Henry’s nephew) had made a failed attempt to invade and take over England. Later, when Henry proposed the marriage of Edward VI to Mary Queen of Scots to unite the British and Scottish thrones at the next generation after them, the Scots disagreed and took Mary over to France for safekeeping. So, angry at this, he thought that if the British and Scottish thrones were united after he died, then the Scots would win, and he didn’t want this.

So, in terms of Henry’s will, the next person in line if Elizabeth I died childless was Frances Brandon, (his niece) and then her daughter Jane Grey. Henry’s will named his successors after Elizabeth as: Fraces, Jane, Katherine, (Edward Seymour – Katherine’s son - wasn’t born yet) Mary, Eleanor and Margaret.



How Edward VI Wanted to Alter the Succession: Edward VI, when he knew he was dying, was urged by his regency council to make his will so that Mary I, a Catholic, would not inherit and disrupt all the progress that Edward had made in terms of making England Protestant. Therefore, he made his will similar to Henry’s where instead of having Mary and then Elizabeth as successors, he skipped over them, and the next person after them in Henry’s will: Frances Brandon and instead named her daughter Jane Grey as heiress. If Jane didn’t have children, the throne was to pass to Katherine Grey and her son, and then to Mary Grey, then Eleanor and Margaret.

The reason why he didn’t name Frances as his successor was that Jane Grey’s father-in-law was John Dudley, and she was married to his son Guilford. Therefore, John urged Edward to name her as his heir, as he thought that that way he would be able to control his son, who would tell Jane what to do, and through them, John would ultimately become King of England in all but name. Though of course this plan failed, and while Jane was declared Queen of England, and Dudley got an army to challenge Mary I, he realized that he was outnumbered by her forces. So, the council, after only nine days on the throne, told Jane that she was no longer queen and ordered John to stand down and proclaim Mary I as the rightful Queen of England, which he did.
