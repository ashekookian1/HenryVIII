Elizabeth I: 1533 - 1603





Childhood and Early Life: 1533 - 1547

Birth: September 7th, 1533 at the Palace of Greenwich, London.

Mother: Anne Boleyn - Henry's second wife.

Childhood: As was the custom at the time, Mary wasn't raised in the same palace that her father was living in. Instead, she was given her own household on one of her father's properties. This was done not only so Henry could focus on his royal duties, but also because of the high risk of disease and infant death at the time. Therefore, she would be kept out of court, and lessen her chances of catching disease and dying prematurely. Her household consisted of a wet nurse, (until her services were no longer needed a year into Mary's life) cleaning staff and cooks among others.

Education: She was tutored in French, Latin, Spanish, Italian, Dutch, Greek and learned how to play the lute. In 1543, her education was overseen by his stepmother Katherine Parr (whom she was very close to) and she ensured that she and her brother Edward were not only given a first-rate education, but they were taught theology by Protesant leaning tutors, as Katherine was a Protestant herself. This would go on to influence their reigns and the religious future of England, as after the death of Mary I, with the exception of James II in the 1600s, England would no longer be a Catholic country, and be united under the Anglican church.

March 23rd, 1534: Parliament passes the Act of Succession, saying that only children born to Henry and Anne would be eligible to be heirs to the throne. It also says that anyone denying that Anne and Henry were legally married, or that she was legally Queen of England, or that Henry was now the head of the Church of England, would be committing treason and subject to execution. This results in quite a few people refusing to take the oath, and being subsequently beheaded. On the same day, the Pope declares Henry's marriage to Katherine and their children legitimate and orders him to banish Anne from court and reinstate Katherine in her place under pain of Henry's excommunication. Henry refuses, thus reinforcing the permanent break of the English Church from Rome (as is still the case today).

May 19th, 1536: Anne Boleyn is beheaded at Tower Green on false charges of committing adultery with five men, including her own brother. The charges were made up as Henry had gotten tired of Anne, wasn't happy that she hadn't given him a son, and was looking for an excuse to be rid of her and marry Jane Seymour - Anne's lady in waiting whom he was currently having an affair with.

May 30th, 1536: Jane Seymour and Henry are married and she is proclaimed Queen on June 4th.



June 8th, 1536: Second Act of Succession - saying that Henry's children with Anne Boleyn and Katherine of Aragon were illegitimate, and any heirs to the throne would come from children he had with Jane Seymour. Elizabeth is now stripped of her title as princess, declared illegitimate, and is now to he known as the Lady Elizabeth. She was banished to one of her father's lesser houses in the countryside, so he would not have to see her and think about what happened to her mother. She spends most of the rest of her childhood there.

1536 - 1543: Henry VIII marries another four times, and Elizabeth is sometimes back at court to visit her stepmothers.



February 7th, 1544: As a thank you to Katherine Parr (his sixth wife) for bringing him closer to his children, and reconciling him with Elizabeth, Henry has Parliament pass the Third and final Act of Succession of his reign. It reinstated Mary and then Elizabeth (though not their former rank as princesses) and any children they might have to the line of succession after Edward and any children he might have.



January 28th, 1547: Henry VIII dies at age 55. Edward, Elizabeth and Mary's half brother, at age 9, is now proclaimed King Edward VI. Henry is buried on February 16th at Windsor Castle next to his former wife Queen Jane (Edward'd mother) at his request.





Life Under Edward VI: 1547 - 1553

February 1547: After Henry died, Thomas Seymour younger brother of Jane Seymour, and therefore uncle to the then current King Edward VI, was back at court, as he was no longer scared of Henry sending him off to as an ambassador to a foreign country. Thomas was ambitious, manipulative, and thought himself to be a ladies man. So he had set his sights on Elizabeth. He sent her love letters, and asked her to marry him. She said no, as she said she was too young and also neeeded time to mourn her father. It was after this refusal, that Thomas then decided to re-pursue the now very rich widow, Katherine Parr, again, (they had dated before Henry made it clear that he wanted to marry her and shipped Thomas off to Europe as a foreign ambassador to get him out of the way) as he thought that her money and influence could get him a place on the regency council where he could control his nephew and thus rule the country.



March 1547: The now 40 year-old Thomas resumes his relationship to 35 year-old Katherine Parr, and after some time has passed, proposes. She accepts, as he is the man most closest to her in age that she has every married, and she also belives that she is in love with him, though at the time doesn't realize that he is using his charm to manipulate getting the rich widow to marry him. Katherine now leaves court and moves to the Old Manor House in the town of Chelsea; one of the properties that was left to her use for the rest of her lifetime by Henry in 1544. (It has since been demolished.)

April 1547: Thomas and Katherine marry in secret at the Old Manor House. They must marry in secret, as officially, Katherine is still supposed to be in mourning for Henry. Also, dowager queens were supposed to wait a few months after their husbands died, to prove that they weren't pregnant by them before remarrying, so that that would stop any potential issues for the line of succession to the throne.



January 1548: Elizabeth was becoming tired of the rigid court etiquette and Katherine, still maintaining her friendship with her despite Henry's death, sought to rescue her from that situation. So she invited her to stay with her and Thomas at the Old Manor. Thomas then proceeds to seduce the 14 year-old Elizabeth once she arrives, as he still hopes that he will get her to marry him, and he can one day become King of England. (He thinks that he will get Katherine to divorce him if the situation comes to it.)

April 1548: Katherine discovers Thomas and Elizabeth together in a compromising situation, and finally realizes Thomas's motives all along. She is devastated, and this causes a rift between her and Thomas for the rest of her life, though they do later try to pretend that all is well for the sake of the impending baby.

May 1548: Elizabeth, due to the situation, leaves and goes to live in her manor house in the town of Cheston, as she doesn't want to go back to court.

August 30, 1548: Katherine gives birth to a daughter: Mary.

September 7th, 1548: Katherine dies at 36 of an infection acquired in childbirth; the same way her sister-in-law Jane Seymour died. She is buried the next day in the chapel of Sudeley Castle. Thomas writes to Elizabeth, attempting to pursue her again, but Elizabeth refuses.

January 16th, 1549: Thomas, jealous of his brother Edward's position at court as Lord Protector, thus defacto king until Edward VI turned 18, wanted to gain control over his nephew and thus the realm. He was caught outside Edward VI's bedroom door with a loaded pistol; doubtless trying to break in and kidnap the king.

January 18th, 1549: Knowing that Thomas had designs on Elizabeth, Edward's regency council sent people to question her to see if Thomas had gotten her to collaborate with him. She denied it.

February 22nd, 1549: The regency council after questioning everyone else assoicated with Thomas, accuses him of treason. He was found guilty by an act of attainder.

March 20th, 1549: He is beheaded for treason at the Tower of London. Elizabeth upon hearing of it said: "Today died a man with much wit and not much judgment.” It is thought that because of the way her father treated her mother, and because of how Thomas treated her, that was why she, later in life, refused to marry.



Life Under Mary I: 1553 - 1558

August 3rd 1553: To celebrate her victory against the regency council and the ouster of Jane Grey as queen, Mary rode triumphantly into London on a wave of popular support, accompanied by Elizabeth and a procession of over 800 nobles and gentlemen.



Early October 1553: Mary declares her parents' marriage to be valid and restores the English church to Catholicism. Elizabeth outwardly conforms, but inside she is still a Protesant.

December 7th 1553: Mary wanting to marry a Catholic prince, so she could have children with him and keep the line of succession Catholic and away from Elizabeth, a Protestant, presented the Privy Council a marriage treaty saying that she wanted to marry Prince Philip of Spain. He was the heir to the throne of Spain and the Holy Roman Empire, and Mary's first cousin once removed.



January - February 1554 - Wyatt's Rebellion: Thomas Wyatt, a poet and popular courtier decides to rise up against Mary's proposed marriage. Most of the English people are behind him in thought, as they don't like Mary marrying a foreign prince, they prefer her to marry someone English. They fear that Philip would not only keep England Catholic again, but bring over the Spanish Inquisition to torture English Protestants. They do not like that Mary has instuted pro-Catholic reforms and imprisoned prominent Protestant clergy. Finally, they thought that Philip would take over England when he became King of Spain, and make it a satellite state of Spain.



Actual Rebellion: Wyatt gathered up some supporters, about 2,500, and planned to attack London. However, they were outnumbered by Mary's much larger forces and captured and executed for treason. Among the people who joined Wyatt and were execured were Henry Grey, Duke of Suffolk, and Jane Grey's father. Elizabeth, though protesting her innocence in the Wyatt affair, was questioned and imprisoned in the Tower of London for two months, then put under house arrest at Woodstock Palace.

July 25th, 1554: Mary and Philip are married at Winchester Cathedral.



September 1554 - July 1555: Mary exhibited signs of what she thought was pregnancy, (Elizabeth was called out of house arrest in April 1555 to witness the birth of a child that would stop her from becoming queen and turning England Protestant again) but was later found to be a false alarm. Most likely early symptoms of the stomach cancer that would later kill her.

January 16th, 1555: After reaching out to the Pope and informing him that she was reuniting the English church with him, Mary had Parliament pass the Heresy Acts, which said that Protesants could be burned at the stake for their beliefs. The first burnings started in February. Mary burned 280 people at the stake over the course of her reign, (including Thomas Cranmer in 1556 - who recanted his Protestantism, so as to free himself from imprisonment, but Mary didn't believe him and burned him anyway - it turned out she was right, as he changed his mind about the recanting right before he was burned at the stake) and therefore earned the nickname: Bloody Mary. This incurred the anger of her people.

1555 - 1558: Elizabeth spends the rest of Mary's reign in her country houses, being out of court and less likely to be accused of taking part in potential plots to take over the throne.

November 17th, 1558: Mary dies, age 42, probably of stomach cancer. Elizabeth, her half sister, at 25, is now Elizabeth I, Queen of England. (She was then known as Queen Elizabeth, and not Elizabeth I, and that would only happen at the ascension of Elizabeth II in 1952.)



Summary of Events of Her Reign: 1558 - 1603 (Too Numerious to Go Into Details)

January 15th, 1559: Coronation at Westminster Abbey



Regligous Reforms - 1558 - 1559: Elizabeth had her Parliament pass laws where she was now the head of the Church of England, broke again with the Pope, and the church was now Protestant. Its services were laid out in the Book of Common Prayer, which was a Protestant service. Also, all people were now ordered to attend Protestant church, though the penalties for refusing to do so weren't heresy or severe. After this, until and including the current day, with the exception of the reign of James II in the 1600s, the English church would remain Protestant.

Marriage Question: As Elizabeth was the last of her father's line, her councilors urged her to get married at have children to assure the succession. She in the end entertained suitors for her hand, but ultimately refused to marry. So was in love with courtier Robert Dudley, but refused to marry him after his wife died in 1560, as he was thought too low of a rank to marry royalty. She also didn't want to risk marrying and having her husband try and usurp her authority and thus govern England. Thus she would be known as "The Virgin Queen".



1567 - Imprisonment of Mary Queen of Scots: Mary, the Queen of Scotland, and Elizabeth's heir, had fled Scotland into England, as her nobles has ousted her, not happy with her marriage. They kept control of her son, James VI of Scotland and made him King. Mary thought Elizabeth would send troops back to Scotland to help her, but she didn't and instead kept her imprisoned for the next 19 years, as Elizabeth was reluctant to go to war, and also didn't want to free her successor, as Mary was Catholic, and this could cause English people against the religious settlement to back Mary so that she would overthrow Elizabeth and gain the throne, restoring Catholicism to England.



1569: A major pro-Catholic uprising in the north of England is put down by Elizabeth's troops.

1570: In response to the uprising, a papal bull was issued stating: "Elizabeth, the pretended Queen of England and the servant of crime" to be excommunicated and a heretic, releasing all her subjects from any allegiance to her." Catholics who obeyed her orders were threatened with excommunication. Thus the Pope was encouraging all Catholic European rulers to overthrow Elizabeth and conquer England, making it Catholic again and putting the Catholic Mary on the throne.

Establishment of Virginia Colony: Elizabeth had sent some of her seafearers to explore the Americas. They landed and named that area: Virgina, after her: the Virgin Queen. The name stuck and is still in use today.

Babington Plot - 1586: After 19 years of imprisonment, Mary sent out a coded letter to her allies in Spain and France agreeing to the assasination of Elizabeth, the invasion of England by them, and putting her on its throne. This letter was intercepted and Mary was put on trial for treason. She was found guilty.

February 8th 1587: Mary is beheaded for treason. Perhaps the most well known execution of Elizabeth's reign.

July 29th, 1588: King Philip II of Spain (Mary's former husband and Elizabeth's former brother-in-law) decides to make good on the papal bull and invade England as revenge for the death of Mary. His ships are defeated by the English naval fire ships - ships that are set on fire and sent in the direction of the Spanish armada. This would mark the most decisive victory in Elizabeth's career, and would deter other countries from trying to invade England, thus establishing it as a major naval power for years to come.



1590s: Literature and theater reached their peak in England, and this is when Shakespeare and Marlowe were writing plays and having them performed at the original Globe Theater. (It burned down and was rebuilt in later years.) Elizabeth was known to attend these plays.

March 24th, 1603: Elizabeth, aged 69, dies and James VI of Scotland (Mary's son) is proclaimed James I of England, now ruling both countries together.

April 28th, 1603: Elizabeth is buried in Westminster Abbey. In 1606, James moves her tomb to share it with her sister Mary's (also in the abbey) as he places his mother Mary's tomb in a prominent position in the abbey, and needs to rearrange things to make space.
