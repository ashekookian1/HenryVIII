Edward VI: 1537 - 1553





Childhood and Early Life: 1537 - 1547

Birth: October 12th, 1537 at Hampton Court Palace.

Mother: Jane Seymour - Henry's third wife.

October 24th, 1537: Jane dies of an infection acquired during childbirth and is buried on November 12th in Winsdor Castle. Henry goes in seclusion mourning her between her death and her funeral, and puts his court into official mourning for her until February 1538.

Childhood: As was the custom at the time, Edward wasn't raised in the same palace that his father was living in. Instead, he was given his own household on one of his father's properties. This was done not only so Henry could focus on his royal duties, but also because of the high risk of disease and infant death at the time. Therefore, he would be kept out of court, and lessen his chances of catching disease and dying prematurely. His household consisted of a wet nurse, (until her services were no longer needed a year into his life) cleaning staff, cooks, musicians to entertain him and general household servants.



1543: At the age of six, Edward's education started. He was taught French, Spanish, Italian, geometry, various sciences, lute playing, and theology. His education was overseen by his stepmother Katherine Parr (whom he was very close to) and she ensured that he and his sister Elizabeth were not only given a first-rate education, but they were taught theology by Protesant leaning tutors, as Katherine was a Protestant herself. This would go on to influence their reigns and the religious future of England, as after the death of Mary I, with the exception of James II in the 1600s, England would no longer be a Catholic country, and be united under the Anglican church.





Reign as King Edward VI: 1547 - 1553

January 23rd, 1547: Henry VIII, knowing that he is dying, announces that his son Edward is to succeed him with a regency council of 16 important nobles to rule for him until he is 18, as Edward is only 9 at the time.

January 28th, 1547: Henry VIII dies at age 55. Edward at age 9, is now proclaimed King Edward VI. Henry is buried on February 16th at Windsor Castle next to his former wife Queen Jane (Edward'd mother) at his request.

February 4th, 1547: The regency council chooses one of their members: Edward Seymour, older brother to Jane Seymour, and thus Edward's uncle, as Lord Protector of England. It is said that he achieved this because he used his power to give the council members grants of land and titles. To illustrate this: he himself at the selection of Lord Protector now gained the noble title of the Duke of Somerset. Henry's will had intended the council to rule by a majority vote on government issues and didn't provide for a Lord Protector (head of the council and ruling in name of Edward until he was 18) role, but Seymour had succeeded in bypassing this and becoming King of England in all but name.




February 20th, 1547: Edward's coronation at Westminster Abbey.

September 1547: Battle of Pinkie in Scotland. Edward Seymour succeeds in defeating the Scotish army. Henry VIII had earlier sent Seymour into England when he proposed a future marriage between Edward VI and Mary Queen of Scots. The Scots rejected this, and angry, Henry sent Seymour to invade and try and capture the Queen by force. While Seymour won some battles, Mary was taken to France for safekeeping, and bethrothed to the French heir to the throne. In 1549, the cost of maintaining an army in Scotland was too great for the crown, so Seymour was forced to withdraw his troops.

1549: Thomas Cranmer, Archbishop of Canterbury and head of the Church of England, now takes it towards a more Protestant direction. He authorizes the printing of the Book of Common Prayer, the book that is used in English church services to this day. As the book is of the Protestant denomination, this does not go down well with some of the English people who are more pro-Catholic. This causes the armed revolt of the Book of Common Prayer in the cities of Corwall and Devon. Seymour has to use major military force to put it down.

January 16th, 1549: Thomas Seymour, younger brother of Edward Seymour, and thus also, Edward VI's uncle, jealous of his brother's position in court, wanted to gain control over his nephew and thus the realm. He was caught outside Edward VI's bedroom door with a loaded pistol; doubtless trying to break in and kidnap the king.



February 22nd, 1549: The regency council after questioning everyone assoicated with Thomas, accuses him of treason. He was found guilty by an act of attainder.

March 20th, 1549: He is beheaded for treason at the Tower of London.

October 1st, 1549: The unpopularity of Edward Seymour's putting down of the religious uprising, along with his also forceful handling and military put down of a farmer's revolt in April 1549, brought the Crown to financial ruin, causing him to lose popularity. Hearing about his unpopularity and that the regency council was about to overthrow him, he took Edward VI and withdrew to the fortified Windsor Castle.

October 11th, 1549: Seymour is arrested and charged.

February 1550: John Dudley, Earl of Warwick now emerges as head of the regency council. After this, Seymour is released from prison and restored to the council, though not as Lord Protector.



1550: The 13-year-old Edward was betrothed to the five-year-old Elisabeth of Valois, daughter of Henry II of France, to be married when she became at least 14.

January 1552: Edward Seymour is beheaded for treason after being caught scheming to overthrow Dudley's rule.



Death and Succession Crisis: 1553

June 1553: Knowing that he was terminally ill (probably of TB) the regency council persuaded Edward to make his will. The will, knowing that his successor would be his half sister Mary, a Catholic, sought to exclude her from the line of succession. It said that the throne would go to Lady Jane Grey , a Protesant, thus keeping England Protestant, and her male children. Jane Grey was the oldest grandchild of Mary Tudor, younger sister of Henry VIII. Jane was also conveniently married to Lord Guilford Dudley, a son of John Dudley. This way, John thought that he could keep control over the throne.





Explanation of Will: Henry's will had stated that if his children didn't have heirs, he wanted their successors to come from the line of his younger sister Mary, discounting the line of his older sister Margaret. (Tradition of male preference primogeniture (how the succession was determined at the time) dictated that it was all male children and their descendants in birth order, followed by all female children and their descendants, again by birth order.) This was because Margaret had married King James IV of Scotland, and therefore, her descendants would inherit the united crowns of Scotland and England. Henry didn't want this, because he had tried to conquer Scotland by force and failed. So if Margaret's children inherited the throne, he saw this as Scotland's revenge, and didn't want it. So in this way, Henry VIII's will was followed, but it skipped over the claims of Edward's sisters Mary and Elizabeth first, before moving on to his aunt's descendants.

July 6th, 1553: Edward dies at 15. He is buried on August 8th at Westminster Abbey.

July 10th, 1553: John Dudley proclaims Jane Grey Queen of England, after taking a few days to gather up his forces. Mary, Edward's sister, hearing that something like this was about to happen, goes to her estates in Norfolk, where she can count on the support of her tenants. She also proclaims herself Queen.

July 14th, 1553: John Dudley leaves London and takes his army with him out to Framlingham Castle in Suffolk where Mary and her loyal forces are. They number 20,000 vs. John's 3,000, as his declaring of Jane Queen proved very unpopular with the English people. Though some didn't like Mary based on her Catholicism, most supported her claim to the throne based on the English laws of succession regardless of her religion.

July 19th, 1553: The ruling Privy Council in London, realizing what was going on, and realizing they were headed for defeat, proclaimed Mary as queen, and told Dudley's army, still not yet arrived to meet Mary's to stop, and for Dudley to proclaim Mary as Queen. He did so.



July 24th, 1553: Dudley is arrested for his part in the revolt.

August 22nd 1553: Dudley is beheaded for treason.
